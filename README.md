# Protection against virulent Clostridioides difficile infection is linked to the metabolic niche breadth of avirulent colonizing strains
## Overview
According to modern coexistence theory, two competing species sharing a nutrient landscape will either coexist or one will competitively exclude the other. Here we show that this ecological dynamic plays out at the strain level within *Clostridioides difficile* and determines whether a host is protected from life-threatening infection. C. difficile infection (CDI) affects nearly 500,000 patients annually in the US, with recurrence in ~30% of cases despite antibiotic treatment. Certain avirulent strains are known to protect the host against secondary virulent challenge, but the mechanisms underlying this protection remain poorly understood. Working with 21 clinical MLST1 isolates from Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center that share ANI >99.84% yet span the full range of virulence, we show that select avirulent strains confer protection against the hypervirulent VPI10463 strain in a mouse colonization model while others do not. Protection is independent of adaptive immunity, microbiome composition, and common genetic determinants including toxin genes, core genome variation, and accessory gene content. BIOLOG phenotype microarray profiling across 2,090 strain-by-metabolite conditions captures the nutrient utilization preferences of each strain and reveals that protective strains can utilize substantially broader carbon sources than non-protective or virulent strains, reflecting a wider metabolic niche. qPCR-based strain-level quantification confirms that ST1.75 competitively excludes VPI10463 from the mouse gut within 3 days post-infection, even when VPI10463 is introduced at a 5-fold inoculum excess. A consumer-resource model parameterized from this data predicts competitive exclusion of VPI10463 by protective strains and coexistence with non-protective strains, consistent with the in vivo observations. These findings establish metabolic niche breadth as the primary determinant of inter-strain competitive dynamics and CDI protection, offering an ecologically grounded framework for the rational design of strain-specific live biotherapeutics against CDI.
